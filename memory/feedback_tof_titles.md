## Title Rules
- Titles must be "sexy" and curiosity-driven, not clinical or technical.
- Reframe technical concepts into intriguing angles. "Elimination Diet Tracker & Symptom Log Template" = bad. "9 Non-Food Factors That May Be Messing Up Your Gut" = good.
- "Decision trees," "trackers," "symptom logs," "self-assessments" sound like homework. Reframe into the surprising insight or outcome.
- Short, punchy titles win. "5-Day Gut Reset Roadmap" > "Our 5-Day Gut Reset Meal Plan & Prep Guide."

**Why:** Technical/clinical titles don't stop the scroll on LinkedIn. The hook needs to create curiosity, not describe a deliverable format.

**How to apply:** After generating any idea, run a "would someone screenshot this title and send it to a friend?" test. If it sounds like a medical form or a project management tool, reframe around the surprising insight inside it.

## Idea Rules
- NEVER throw shade at other professionals (doctors, other practitioners). No "myths your doctor missed" or "what your doctor got wrong" framing.
- Professional practitioners (dietitians, doctors, coaches) generally do NOT want to give away recipes or detailed meal plans as giveaways — that feels commoditized. Instead, reframe around mistakes, frameworks, or insider knowledge.
- Avoid ideas with very high item counts (47+, 25+) for practitioners — these feel like content dumps, not expert artifacts. Curated small numbers (5-9) feel more authoritative from a professional.
- Don't suggest ideas that aren't relevant to the practitioner's expertise level. A dietitian sharing "anti-bloat meal ideas" is generic Instagram content, not a professional lead magnet.

**Why:** The giveaway must reinforce the practitioner's authority and expertise. Generic content (recipes, meal ideas) commoditizes them. Shade at peers damages professional reputation.

**How to apply:** For health/wellness professionals, lean toward: surprising factors, common mistakes, decision frameworks, proprietary protocols, and "things most people don't know" angles. Avoid: recipe collections, meal plans as the main offer, and any framing that pits the practitioner against peers.
