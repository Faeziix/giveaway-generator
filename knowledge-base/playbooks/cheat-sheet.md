# Cheat Sheet Playbook

## What It Is
A cheat sheet is a condensed, quick-reference document that distills a complex topic into its most essential information. It is designed to be saved, bookmarked, or printed and kept within arm's reach. Unlike a guide that teaches, a cheat sheet assumes some baseline knowledge and serves as a shortcut to execution.

## When to Use It
- **Buyer stage:** Top-of-funnel (awareness) and mid-funnel (consideration)
- **Best industries:** Agencies, marketing teams, SaaS, e-commerce, any field with technical terminology or processes
- **Taxonomy categories:** Educate
- **Use when:** Your audience repeatedly looks up the same information, there are rules/specs/shortcuts that are hard to memorize, or you want to create a resource people reference weekly (high retention value)

## Anatomy of a Good Cheat Sheet
- **Title:** States the topic and implies speed/convenience
- **Topic overview:** 2-3 sentences explaining what this cheat sheet covers and who it is for
- **Sections with headers:** Organized by logical category (not just a wall of text)
- **Key info in tables or bullet points:** Data-dense formatting that is scannable, not readable like prose
- **Quick-reference format:** Definitions, formulas, specs, keyboard shortcuts, or rules of thumb
- **Fits on 1-2 pages ideally:** If it requires scrolling through 10 pages, it is a guide, not a cheat sheet
- **Visual hierarchy:** Bold headers, consistent formatting, clear separation between sections

## Step-by-Step Creation Process
1. **Pick one topic** -- Choose a subject where your audience wastes time looking things up. The best cheat sheets replace a Google search habit.
2. **Gather the essential info** -- Collect every fact, spec, shortcut, formula, or rule relevant to the topic. Pull from your own expertise, documentation, and common questions your audience asks.
3. **Ruthlessly compress** -- Cut anything that is not reference-worthy. If someone would only read it once, it does not belong on a cheat sheet. Keep only what people need to look up repeatedly.
4. **Organize into scannable sections** -- Group related info under clear headers. Use tables for comparisons or specs. Use bullet points for lists of rules or tips.
5. **Design for glanceability** -- Use bold text, color coding, or icons to make critical info pop. The reader should find what they need in under 5 seconds.
6. **Name it** -- Apply naming conventions from naming-conventions.md. Signal that this is a quick-reference resource.
7. **Package and deliver** -- Format as a designed PDF (1-2 pages). Consider a landscape orientation for desk printing. Offer a digital version for saving to phone or desktop.

## Naming Tips
- **Formula 1:** "The [Topic] Cheat Sheet" -- e.g., *The Facebook Ad Specs Cheat Sheet*
- **Formula 2:** "[Topic]: The Complete Cheat Sheet for [Audience]" -- e.g., *Whitelisting: The Complete Cheat Sheet for Media Buyers*
- **Formula 3:** "The Ultimate [Topic] Quick Reference" -- e.g., *The Ultimate Ad Creative Variations Quick Reference*

See naming-conventions.md for full naming guidelines.

## Linear Examples
- **Whitelisting Cheat Sheet** -- Works because whitelisting is a process most media buyers do regularly but few have fully memorized. High repeat-use value.
- **Unlimited Ad Variations Cheat Sheet** -- Works because it promises a creative shortcut (variations) in a scannable format. The word "unlimited" creates high perceived value.
- **Q4 Creative Strategy Cheatsheet** -- Works because it ties to a specific, time-sensitive moment (Q4) when urgency is high and people want fast answers.

## Effort Estimate
- **Time to create:** 2-4 hours
- **Skills needed:** Writing, basic design (layout and formatting)
- **Maintenance:** One-time, unless the content is seasonal or tied to platform specs that change (then update periodically)
