# Template Collection Playbook

## What It Is
A template collection is a curated set of ready-to-use structures, formats, or frameworks that the reader can immediately copy and adapt for their own work. Rather than teaching theory, it hands people the actual skeleton they need to produce output faster. The value is in the breadth and organization of options.

## When to Use It
- **Buyer stage:** Mid-funnel (consideration) and bottom-funnel (decision)
- **Best industries:** Agencies, creative teams, marketing departments, content creators, freelancers
- **Taxonomy categories:** Accelerate
- **Use when:** Your audience creates similar assets repeatedly and needs starting points, you want to showcase your methodology through real structures, or the task is more about execution speed than knowledge gaps

## Anatomy of a Good Template Collection
- **Title with number:** The number of templates is a core part of the value proposition (bigger = more perceived value)
- **Intro explaining how to use the collection:** 1-2 paragraphs on how to pick the right template, how to customize, and what results to expect
- **Categorized templates:** Grouped by use case, goal, or style so the reader can quickly navigate to what they need
- **Each template includes:**
  - Name (memorable, descriptive)
  - Structure or formula (the actual template with placeholders)
  - Example (a filled-in version showing the template in action)
  - When to use it (1-2 sentences on the ideal scenario for this template)
- **Design:** Clean formatting that clearly separates each template. Consider color coding by category.

## Step-by-Step Creation Process
1. **Audit your existing work** -- Review your past projects, client work, and internal processes. Identify patterns and structures you use repeatedly. These are your templates hiding in plain sight.
2. **Define categories** -- Group your templates by use case, funnel stage, content type, or any other axis that helps the reader self-select. Aim for 3-6 categories.
3. **Build each template** -- For every template, write out the structure with clear placeholders (e.g., "[Brand Name]", "[Pain Point]", "[Desired Outcome]"). Strip away anything specific to one client or project.
4. **Add examples** -- Fill in each template with a realistic example. This is what makes a template collection actually useful -- people need to see it in action to understand how to adapt it.
5. **Write the "when to use" context** -- For each template, add 1-2 sentences explaining the ideal scenario. This prevents people from using the wrong template and getting poor results.
6. **Name it** -- Apply naming conventions from naming-conventions.md. Lead with the number of templates.
7. **Package and deliver** -- Format as a PDF for browsing, but also consider offering editable versions (Google Docs, Notion, Figma) so people can actually use the templates without retyping.

## Naming Tips
- **Formula 1:** "[Number]+ [Type] Templates" -- e.g., *60+ Winning Video Ad Templates*
- **Formula 2:** "The [Audience] Template Pack: [Number] [Type] Templates" -- e.g., *The Media Buyer Template Pack: 40 Ad Copy Templates*
- **Formula 3:** "[Type] Templates That [Outcome]" -- e.g., *Creative End Card Templates That Drive Clicks*

See naming-conventions.md for full naming guidelines.

## Linear Examples
- **60+ Winning Video Ad Templates** -- Works because the large number (60+) signals comprehensive coverage, and "winning" implies these are proven, not theoretical.
- **Creative End Card Templates** -- Works because it targets a very specific asset (end cards) that creative teams produce constantly but rarely optimize.

## Effort Estimate
- **Time to create:** 4-8 hours
- **Skills needed:** Writing, design/formatting (to make templates visually clear and easy to follow)
- **Maintenance:** One-time (add new templates over time as a content refresh strategy, but the initial collection stands on its own)
