# Framework Collection Playbook

## What It Is
A framework collection is a curated set of repeatable mental models, strategic structures, or formulas that help the reader approach a category of problems systematically. Unlike templates (which give you the exact structure to fill in), frameworks give you the thinking model to create your own structures. A framework collection says: "Here are 9 different ways to think about this problem -- pick the one that fits your situation."

## When to Use It
- **Buyer stage:** Mid-funnel (consideration) and bottom-funnel (decision)
- **Best industries:** Agencies, consultancies, marketing teams, content creators, strategists
- **Taxonomy categories:** Accelerate
- **Use when:** Your audience faces a category of problems that can be solved multiple ways (e.g., writing copy, structuring ads, building strategies), you want to demonstrate strategic depth and versatility, or the audience is experienced enough to adapt a framework to their own context

## Anatomy of a Good Framework Collection
- **Title with number:** The count of frameworks is a key value signal
- **Intro on when and why to use frameworks:** 1-2 paragraphs explaining the value of having multiple frameworks at your disposal and how to choose the right one for a given situation
- **Each framework includes:**
  - Name (memorable, ideally branded or proprietary-sounding)
  - Structure/formula (the actual framework laid out step by step or as a formula)
  - Example (the framework applied to a real scenario so the reader can see it in action)
  - When to use it (1-2 sentences on the ideal context for this framework)
- **Categorization:** If the collection is large (10+), group frameworks by use case, difficulty, or goal
- **Comparison guide:** A summary table or decision tree that helps the reader pick the right framework for their situation

## Step-by-Step Creation Process
1. **Identify the problem category** -- What type of work does your audience do repeatedly that benefits from structured thinking? Copywriting, ad creative, strategy development, and campaign planning are strong candidates.
2. **Audit your own frameworks** -- Review your internal processes, client work, and training materials. Identify the mental models your team already uses. Most experts operate from frameworks without having formally named them.
3. **Name and formalize each framework** -- Give each framework a memorable name. Write out the structure as a formula, step sequence, or acronym. The name and structure are what make it shareable and sticky.
4. **Build examples for each** -- Apply each framework to a realistic scenario. Show the inputs, the framework in action, and the output. Without examples, frameworks remain abstract and unusable.
5. **Write the "when to use" guidance** -- For each framework, explain the ideal scenario. Help the reader self-select instead of trying all of them randomly.
6. **Name it** -- Apply naming conventions from naming-conventions.md. Lead with the number and signal the type of thinking the collection enables.
7. **Package and deliver** -- A designed PDF works well for browsing. Consider also offering a Notion database or interactive web page where users can filter frameworks by use case. Include a one-page "cheat sheet" summary of all frameworks for quick reference.

## Naming Tips
- **Formula 1:** "Our Top [Number] [Topic] Frameworks" -- e.g., *Our Top 9 Copywriting Frameworks*
- **Formula 2:** "[Number] [Adjective] [Topic] Frameworks" -- e.g., *25 High Performing UGC Frameworks*
- **Formula 3:** "The [Audience] Framework Toolkit: [Number] Models for [Outcome]" -- e.g., *The Creative Strategist Framework Toolkit: 15 Models for Better Ads*

See naming-conventions.md for full naming guidelines.

## Linear Examples
- **Our Top 9 Copywriting Frameworks** -- Works because "our top 9" implies these are battle-tested (not theoretical), and copywriting frameworks have universal appeal across marketing roles.
- **25 High Performing UGC Frameworks** -- Works because the large number (25) signals comprehensive coverage, "high performing" implies data-backed selection, and UGC is a high-demand topic.

## Effort Estimate
- **Time to create:** 4-8 hours (depending on the number of frameworks and depth of examples)
- **Skills needed:** Writing, strategic thinking (ability to articulate mental models clearly and provide useful examples)
- **Maintenance:** One-time. Frameworks are conceptual and do not go stale. Add new frameworks over time as a content refresh strategy.
