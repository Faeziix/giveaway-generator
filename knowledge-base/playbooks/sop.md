# SOP Playbook

## What It Is
A Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is a detailed, step-by-step document that describes exactly how to execute a specific process from start to finish. Unlike a checklist (which reminds you what to do), an SOP tells you how to do each step, including decision points, scripts, templates, and troubleshooting guidance. SOPs make processes repeatable across team members and reduce dependency on any single person's knowledge.

## When to Use It
- **Buyer stage:** Mid-funnel (consideration) and bottom-funnel (decision)
- **Best industries:** Agencies, e-commerce operations, production teams, any business with multi-step workflows involving multiple people
- **Taxonomy categories:** Accelerate
- **Use when:** Your audience manages teams that need to execute processes consistently, there is a complex workflow with decision points and edge cases, or you want to demonstrate deep operational expertise that positions you as a trusted partner

## Anatomy of a Good SOP
- **Title:** Names the specific process (not a general topic)
- **Overview/purpose:** 2-3 sentences explaining what this SOP covers, who should use it, and what outcome it produces
- **Prerequisites:** What needs to be in place before starting (tools, access, information, approvals)
- **Step-by-step numbered process:** Detailed instructions for each step, written so someone unfamiliar with the process could follow along
- **Decision points:** Clearly marked moments where the executor needs to choose between paths, with criteria for each choice
- **Templates/scripts to use at each step:** Embedded or linked resources (email scripts, brief templates, naming conventions) that the executor uses during the process
- **Troubleshooting section:** Common problems that arise at each stage, with solutions
- **Design:** Clear numbering, indentation for sub-steps, visual callouts for decision points and warnings

## Step-by-Step Creation Process
1. **Document the process live** -- The next time you or your team executes this process, document every single action in real time. Do not rely on memory. Screen-record if possible. This captures the steps people actually take, not the steps they think they take.
2. **Identify decision points** -- Review your documentation and mark every moment where someone needs to make a judgment call. For each decision point, write the criteria for choosing each path.
3. **Write the steps in sequence** -- Turn your raw documentation into clear, numbered steps. Each step should start with a verb and include enough detail that a competent person unfamiliar with the process could execute it. Include screenshots where visual guidance helps.
4. **Embed supporting resources** -- At each step that requires a template, script, or tool, link or embed the resource directly. The executor should never need to hunt for something mid-process.
5. **Add the troubleshooting section** -- List the most common things that go wrong and how to fix them. Pull from your team's experience and past mistakes.
6. **Name it** -- Apply naming conventions from naming-conventions.md. Name it after the process, not the outcome.
7. **Package and deliver** -- A Google Doc or Notion page works well (easy to update, easy to share). PDF works for distribution as a lead magnet. Include a version number and last-updated date so users know the SOP is current.

## Naming Tips
- **Formula 1:** "[Process Name] SOP" -- e.g., *Customer Generated Content SOP*
- **Formula 2:** "The [Team/Role] SOP for [Process]" -- e.g., *The Creative Team SOP for UGC Production*
- **Formula 3:** "How to [Process]: The Complete SOP" -- e.g., *How to Run Influencer Whitelisting: The Complete SOP*

See naming-conventions.md for full naming guidelines.

## Linear Examples
- **Customer Generated Content SOP** -- Works because it documents a process (sourcing and managing customer-generated content) that most brands handle ad hoc. Turning it into a formal SOP signals operational maturity and gives teams a system to follow immediately.

## Effort Estimate
- **Time to create:** 4-8 hours (more if the process has many decision branches or embedded templates)
- **Skills needed:** Writing, process documentation (ability to observe and articulate workflows clearly)
- **Maintenance:** Periodic updates as the process evolves, tools change, or edge cases are discovered. Include a review cadence (e.g., quarterly) in the SOP itself.
